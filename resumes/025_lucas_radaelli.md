Code readability is  important. Optimization is fun.

I am a software engineer and speaker, and how that came to be is a bit unusual.

I was in high school, apparently certain that I would become a lawyer. I knew a lot of blind people, like me, who were successful judges, lawyers, and I thought I could be the same. Then I met a teacher, Rubens Ferronato, who was inventing a system called multiplano to teach geometry and graphics for the blind.

We started studying together. Instead of learning things that I was studying at school, we started doing something more.   Exercises for the Math Olympiad or subjects part of a math's degree.

I enjoyed my time with this teacher. In high school, my interests increased. Not only literature, arts and history, but also math, physics and programming.

that was when I decided that I would study computer science, where the ultimate goal was to be able to improve the lives of people with disabilities through technology. I was, and still am, using computers, smartphones and technology solutions to solve all the problems that my disability brings. And it has been working. The barriers look more like fences now. I hope I can bring them down to steps, or maybe only a flat surface.

After college, I went to work for Google, developing the Search Ranking algorithm for Google's search engine. I also worked with the infrastructure of the search stack, data mining, developed signals for machine learning models and wrote multiple pipelines for processing data.

I worked in the Fuchsia accessibility team, developing the accessibility stack for this operating system. I was the techlead for the accessibility team during this time. We developed a new accessibility stack from the ground up.

At the moment, I am working with the Chrome accessibility team, improving accessibility performance in the browser as well as helping to implement web standards for accessibility.

As a speaker, I talk about accessibility, inclusion, disability rights and ways we can make our world better for all people, regardless of their disabilities. I believe that the technical decisions we take at the code level can affect lives, and by being aware of the different types of people that will use our products and algorithms, we can make this experience better for everyone.
